CONNECT — THE APP: DESIGN RATIONALE
Each feature, and why it exists.
================================================================

THE THREE NUDGES (from the document):
  Nudge 1 — Social uncertainty  → make peer behaviour visible (social proof)
  Nudge 2 — Choice overload     → personalised ranking + grouping
  Nudge 3 — Present bias        → soft RSVP + morning-of notification

THE SIX DESIGN PRINCIPLES:
  1. Order      — rank by social relevance; tonight before next week; rank, don't hide
  2. Grouping   — sections, not one long undifferentiated list
  3. Headings   — language does the emotional work; never read as "I'm lonely"
  4. Salience   — low-effort action dominant; momentum over raw headcounts; no dopamine-bait
  5. Descriptions — vivid, concrete, real numbers
  6. Decision effort — scannable in seconds; the app has done the sorting


1. ONBOARDING (Welcome → Name → Department → Country → Interests)
----------------------------------------------------------------
Profile collects course, country, and interests — and nothing else.
Why: these are the only three fields the nudges need. Course drives
"X from your course are going," country drives "X from your country are
going" for international students, interests drives "X share your
interests" and interest-based ranking (Nudge 1 & 2). Kept to one short
profile so the student never has to search or filter again (EAST: Easy).

Welcome screen leads with privacy (private by default · you choose what
others see · no data shared externally).
Why: the safety/credibility barrier has to drop before a self-conscious
student commits.

Name is optional ("only if you choose to share it").
Why: keeps identity opt-in and anonymity intact.


2. THE FEED — RANKING & GROUPING (Nudge 2)
----------------------------------------------------------------
Two sections: "MOST RELEVANT TO YOU" and "EVERYTHING ELSE."
Why (principle 2): a long undifferentiated list triggers choice paralysis;
a short curated set makes the social proof land instead of feeling empty.

Personalised ranking: coursemate count (highest weight), then shared
interests, then country, then time proximity.
Why (Nudge 2 + principle 1): events peers are attending, and events
tonight, surface first. The app does the cognitive work, not the student.

Sort toggle (For you / By date / A–Z); events are never removed.
Why (ethics + principle 1): "rank, don't hide" — ordering may change,
availability never does.

Skipped events sink to the bottom of their section and stay reversible.
Why: keeps the experience soft and no-commitment.

Heading "Most relevant to you" (not "Meet people").
Why (principle 3): "Meet people" reads as an admission of loneliness;
"Most relevant to you" normalises attendance as ordinary behaviour.

"Less relevant to you" label removed from the lower section.
Why: it read as algorithmic rejection. Dimming alone signals lower
priority without making anyone feel sorted into a leftover pile.


3. THE EVENT CARD
----------------------------------------------------------------
Card splits identity (on the photo) from social + actions (on the white).
Why (principles 5 & 6): keeps the card scannable in seconds.

Real activity photo, with title, time/location, and a Free/Paid pill over
a soft gradient.
Why (principle 5): vivid and concrete, all the "what / when / where" in
one glance, not an abstract list item.

Badge top-left ("Top pick for you", "Matches your interests", "Your
country").
Why (Nudge 2): states WHY the event surfaced, reducing cognitive load.

"X joined today" pill top-right (momentum), and raw total-attendee numbers
removed everywhere.
Why (principle 4 + anxiety-safe): "8 joined today" feels alive; a big
crowd number ("210 going") intimidates an anxious first-year.

Social proof: "34 from your course are going" / "11 share your interests,"
with initial-avatars.
Why (Nudge 1, Cialdini): social proof is strongest from similar people —
"from your course" lands harder than a generic headcount.

Avatars use tonal shades of one colour, randomised initials.
Why: reads as one cohesive group, and never exposes real identities.

Card kept visually calm, minimal colour.
Why (principle 4): no flashing / dopamine-bait; attention goes to the
proof and the action, not decoration.


4. THE BUTTONS (Nudge 3 + principle 4)
----------------------------------------------------------------
"I might go" is the soft RSVP and the dominant action; Chat and Skip are
compact icons beside it.
Why (Nudge 3 + principle 4): the low-effort, no-commitment action is the
obvious one; the others stay secondary.

"I might go" is a soft tint at rest, solid only when selected.
Why: a loud saturated button at rest reads as pressure. Gentle invitation
until acted on, then it confirms — quiet → committed.

Skip is one tap and fully reversible.
Why: keeps it low-stakes — no penalty, no guilt, no visible flaking.

Detail screen expands to four soft states (I might go / I'm going / Not
for me / Save for later).
Why: full control over intention without a hard commitment.


5. THE MORNING NOTIFICATION (Nudge 3)
----------------------------------------------------------------
"Good morning, [Name] — Tonight: Postgrad Mixer at 6pm. You said you
might go; 34 from your course are attending. Tap to confirm your spot."
Why: morning timing shortens the distance to the payoff while the event
still feels concrete. Too early feels abstract; too late and plans have
formed. This overcomes present bias.


6. PRIVACY & ETHICS
----------------------------------------------------------------
Anonymous by default (stated on the first onboarding screen).
"Show my name to others" — toggle, OFF by default (opt-in name sharing).
"Allow others to message me" — toggle, OFF by default (the document's
"can choose if they want to be contacted").
People tab shows who's going, but contact is opt-in.
No data shared externally.
Why: anonymity is the default; the student opts IN to visibility.


7. ANXIETY-SAFE BY DESIGN
----------------------------------------------------------------
Avoided on purpose, because the users are lonely and often anxious:
  • No scarcity countdowns ("only 3 spots left").
  • No leaderboards or "most social" gamification.
  • No "who declined" / visible flaking.
  • No read receipts.
  • No raw crowd sizes — relative, similar-peer counts only.
  • No flashing, no dopamine-bait, no infinite scroll.
Why: these patterns would make the exact people the app is for feel worse.
